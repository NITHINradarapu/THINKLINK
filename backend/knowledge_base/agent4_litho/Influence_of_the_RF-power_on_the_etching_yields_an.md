# Influence of the RF-power on the etching yields and surface morphology in reactive ion beam etching of Si and photoresist

### ABSTRACT 

The change in ion energy distribution and composition of a reactive ion beam produced by an RF-excited ion beam source and operation with a mixture of CHF3 and O2 was investigated and correlated with the etching behavior. To this end, measurements were performed with an energy-selective mass spectrometer to determine ion energy distributions, current density measurements for the measurement of current density distributions of the ion beam, and tactile measurements to determine the etching rates of Si and photoresist. The morphology of the photoresist was measured with a scanning force microscope. In particular, alterations in the etching yield and surface morphology of the photoresist can be observed in response to changes in the applied RF-power. An increase in plasma density leads to an increase in fragmentation processes of the injected reactive gases, resulting in the formation of smaller fragments. These smaller fragments have a chemical impact on the substrate surface, which affects the etching performance. These effects can have significant consequences in the context of long-time reactive ion beam processing for patterning applications.

### I. INTRODUCTION 

The developments in the manufacturing industry for ultrasmooth and ultraprecise surfaces are constantly growing. The goal of reactive ion beam etching is the exact transfer of a desired shape into a substrate with a resulting surface roughness in the sub-nm range. To attain these high levels of accuracy, several techniques of ion beam processing have been refined over the past few decades. One such application is the transfer of structures into a substrate material, which is exemplified by the manufacturing of optical gratings in SiO2,<sup>1,2</sup> the etching of slanted gratings for augmented reality,<sup>3–5</sup> and synchrotron gratings.<sup>6</sup> Here, ion beam etching is the method of choice. In addition, ion beam figuring is used for the final shape correction of telescope mirrors<sup>7,8</sup> or the manufacturing of microelectronics, such as bulk-acoustic wave filters.<sup>9</sup> By exchanging the inert process gases with reactive gases, such as CF4, CHF3, O2, SF6, or mixtures thereof, even more possibilities arise for the fine-tuning of the process parameters. Nevertheless, due to the fact that molecular gases are used, many different fragmentation 

processes play important roles in the plasma processes, i.e., in the discharge itself but after the extraction of the ions, as well. Because the different fragments have different influences on the ion beam process due to their properties, e.g., ion mass, or chemical reactivity, the fragmentation has a crucial impact on ion beam processing. Especially in terms of reproducibility and transfer of processes from one ion beam etching system to another, a quantitative determination of the ion beam composition can be one key method. A correlation of the change in the etching characteristics, such as removal rates, removal profile shapes, or changes in the surface morphology of substrate or mask, over very long service lives or process times, i.e., several tens of hours, is of major interest. This is one major influence that has to be considered in terms of reproducibility and precise manufacturing. Due to the conditioning effects of the ion beam source and erosion processes, especially of the extraction grid system, a significant change in the applied RF-power is required for keeping the ion beam current constant. This is accompanied by a change in the tool function of the ion beam. Our first work investigated the main physical effects and

Mayer and Barker<sup>12</sup> investigated the change of the applied magnetic field to the discharge of a Kaufman-type ion beam source for the operation with CF4, leading to a distortion of the electron trajectory, i.e., prolongation of the flight path. This increases the possibility of interactions with the neutral gas or ions. They reported an increase in the fragmentation processes inside the discharge. Dornberg et al.<sup>13</sup> also investigated a Kaufman-type ion beam source operated with a mixture of CHF3 and O2 and a variation of different process parameters, such as the feeding gas composition, discharge current, and magnetic field configuration. They correlated these changes with a change in the etching performance of SiO2 and a photoresist (PR). Nevertheless, there is still a lack of information on the operation of RF-excited ion beam sources with reactive gases or even a mixture thereof. A change in the applied RF-power to the plasma discharge leads to a change in the plasma density distribution. This change in plasma parameters is expected to result in a change in the composition of the plasma and, therefore, in a change of the ion beam composition, as well. As already described in the literature, different ions penetrating the substrate and, especially the PR, lead to a different surface chemistry.<sup>14</sup> This results in different etching rates and surface morphologies. In this publication, measurements of the ion beam composition with an energy-selective mass spectrometer are accompanied by etching experiments. The resulting etching rates and surface morphologies are correlated with the ionic composition of the ion beam for a systematic investigation. 

### II. EXPERIMENTAL APPARATUS

### A. Current density measurements 

First, the influence of the applied RF-power on the current density distributions of the ion beam was investigated. In contrast to all other measurements, the extraction voltages of the plasma bridge neutralizer were turned off. Otherwise, the ion beam would get charge neutralized and would not be detectable in a Faraday cup, since the incoming current is, ideally, equal to zero. For every RF-power PRF, three measurements before and after the etching experiments were recorded in order to take the change in the ion beam tool function during an etching process into account. After the measurement, every current density distribution was approximated with a designated regression function. With the aid of the analytical functions, a peak current density (full symbols) in the center of the ion beam and the width of the ion beam, quantified by the FWHM (half symbols), were determined. These data are depicted in Fig. 6. The data points represent the arithmetic average of the six measurements and the error bar is given by their standard deviation.

### IV. DISCUSSION 

While the trends in the changes in current density and etching profiles with RF-power are generally consistent, minor variations can be observed, particularly in the evolution of the width of the etching profile between Si and the photoresist. Given that the change in FWHM is significantly smaller for the photoresist than for Si, it is necessary to consider not only a purely physical origin for the change in the etching profile resulting from a different current density profile, but also other factors. This is also indicated by the change in etching yield for the two different materials. While both etching yields decrease with increasing RF power, the slopes of the two curves seen in Fig. 10 are different. As chemically reactive species are involved in this ion beam process, the two different slopes can be explained by a change in the composition of the ion beam. The ESMS measurements support this assumption. They revealed that a higher RF power leads to more fragmentation of the process gases in the plasma discharge itself. This results in a change in the proportion of incoming species on the substrate. While the fraction of C<sup>þ</sup> ions originating from processes outside of the ion beam source is decreasing, the total count rate of these fragmented and, therefore, slower C<sup>þ</sup> ions is increasing for a higher RF-power. Tachi et al.<sup>18</sup> measured the different etching yields of a mass-selected ion beam consisting of CF<sup>þ</sup> n<sup>speciesforn ¼ 0,. . . , 4</sup> on Si. The results demonstrated that the etching yield is higher for the heavier ions. For the ion energies relevant in this publication, the etching yield can potentially also become negative, i.e., light and slow components of the ion beam lead to a film deposition on the Si substrate. Since the light carbon ions have a lower etching yield on Si than the other ions with a higher mass, the etching yield decreases. Additionally, the change in the ionic composition of the ion beam affects the surface morphology of the photoresist.

As demonstrated by Engelmann et al., the evolution of surface roughness of the photoresist in fluorocarbon plasmas is also dependent on the chemical composition of the plasma, particularly the F=C-ratio on the surface.<sup>14,20,21</sup> While energetic ion bombardment leads to damage, a C-rich layer in the photoresist with a thickness of several nanometers is formed by the energetic ion bombardment. The fluorocarbon ions and radicals lead to the formation of an additional fluorine-carbon layer on top. Its thickness is expected to be dependent on the composition of the ion beam and the composition of the reactive background gas. In this study, only the ionic composition was measured. As reported by Tachi et al., an increase in the RF power is expected to result in a thicker fluorocarbon layer. Since the evolution of photoresist roughness is a result of stress relief in the damaged layer in the photoresist, a thicker fluorocarbon layer on top may lead to a mechanically more stable surface. Consequently, less roughness is expected to be transferred into the photoresist surface as a result of the ion beam etching process.

### V. CONCLUSIONS 

The alteration in the etching behavior resulting from a variation in the applied RF power leads to several conclusions. While the conventional approach for enhancing the removal rate in the etching process is through an increase in the extracted ion current by increasing the RF power, it is essential to consider the potential for a decrease in the etching yield. Despite maintaining the same process gas, a noticeable increase in the surface roughening of the photoresist can be observed with a reduction in the RF power. This must be taken into account, particularly when processing samples over an extended period. As the screen grid of the extraction system degrades, the required RF power for the discharge is reduced in order to keep the ion beam current constant during processing. Consequently, besides changes in etching yield, also changes in the surface morphology have to be expected and taken into account to achieve the best results for the pattern transfer. Nevertheless, ion beam processing has the advantage of a multitude of tunable process parameters, which can be adjusted independently for optimization. A further logical step in the understanding of the reactive ion beam etching process would be to vary, for example, the process gas mixture, process pressure, or ion energy.
