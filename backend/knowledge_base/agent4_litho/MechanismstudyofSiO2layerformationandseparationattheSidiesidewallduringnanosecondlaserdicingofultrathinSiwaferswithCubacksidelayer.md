# **Mechanism study of  SiO2 layer formation and separation at the Si die sidewall during nanosecond laser dicing of ultrathin Si wafers with Cu backside layer** 

### **Michael Raj Marks**<sup>**1,2**</sup> **· Foo Khong Yong**<sup>**1**</sup> **· Kuan Yew Cheong**<sup>**2**</sup> **· Zainuriah Hassan**<sup>**3**</sup>

### **Abstract** 

Laser dicing of ultrathin dies is promising and is gaining importance because of its cost and quality advantages over mechanical and plasma dicing. However, the effects of laser dicing on the mechanical strength and microstructure of ultrathin Si dies need to be further understood, especially when dicing through Si wafers with backside Cu layer. A critical phenomenon effecting the Si die sidewall strength after nanosecond laser dicing of Si wafers with backside Cu is the formation and separation of a  SiO2 layer at the sidewall. The mechanisms behind the  SiO2 layer formation and separation were studied in this work. Si wafer samples without and with backside Cu layer were prepared by dicing with nanosecond laser using standard production parameters. The microstructure and phases formed were investigated by energy dispersive spectroscopy and nanobeam diffraction in a transmission electron microscope. In die samples without backside Cu, the sidewall consists of a thin surface layer of amorphous Si, followed by a polycrystalline Si layer, and finally an epitaxial Si layer. In die samples with backside Cu, the sidewall microstructure was observed to be vastly different. At the upper region of the sidewall, a surface layer of polycrystalline Cu was found, followed by a polycrystalline  Cu3Si layer, a  SiO2 layer mixed with  Cu3Si, and finally a thick SiO2 layer. The  Cu3Si catalyzes the growth of the  SiO2 through an oxidation step of the  Cu3Si on the sidewall surface as well as at the  SiO2/Si interface. In the lower region of the sidewall, the microstructure is similar to the upper region, but there is a separation of the  SiO2 layer from the crystalline Si. The  SiO2 undergoes a decomposition reaction at the  SiO2/Si interface, releasing volatile SiO which causes microvoids to form and grow laterally at the interface. The growth and coalescence of the microvoids eventually lead to the separation of the  SiO2 layer from the crystalline Si, leaving behind a clean and rough crystalline Si surface with a peak-to-peak roughness of 100–200 nm. In the areas where the  SiO2 layer has separated from the Si die sidewall, the fracture strength of the sidewall is dependent on the material property and surface roughness of the crystalline Si, and not on the  SiO2 layer. In the sidewall region near the die frontside, the  SiO2 thickness is more than regions near the die backside, and no microvoiding and separation at the  SiO2/Si interface were detected. This is hypothesized to be due to a higher  O2 pressure at the upper region of the narrow dicing trench which is open to the atmosphere compared to the lower regions where there could be  O2 deprivation and lower  O2 pressure.

## **1 Introduction** 

The application of ultrathin die technology is rapidly growing in the fields of thin packaging, 3D stack packaging and flexible electronics. Ultrathin dies enable significant improvements in device performance by reducing the junction-to-case thermal resistance, ON-resistance  (RON), parasitic effects, as well as increasing RF efficiency. Mechanical handling and stress cause serious problems in the packaging assembly process for ultrathin dies. A die backside

metallization layer such as Cu, acting as a mechanical stabilization layer, is crucial in preventing die warpage and fractures especially during the die attach and wire bonding processes [1]. 

However, the dicing of ultrathin Si wafers with a backside Cu layer presents various challenges in manufacturing. Mechanical blade dicing has a significant risk of causing die cracks owing to blade clogging and damage when cutting through the Cu layer. The cost of plasma dicing is very high owing to the requirement of photolithography steps to etch Si and metals in the dicing street, in addition to the slow etch rate of metals. Laser dicing is promising and it is gaining more importance in the manufacturing of thin semiconductor wafers because of its cost and quality advantages over mechanical and plasma dicing [1]. However, many effects from laser dicing on the mechanical strength and microstructure of the Si die need to be further understood, especially when dicing through Si and metal layers simultaneously. A critical phenomenon effecting the Si die sidewall strength, involving the formation and separation of a Si oxide layer at the die sidewall, has been observed during nanosecond laser dicing of Si wafers with a backside Cu layer [2, 3]. The Si oxide layer is brittle and has a low fracture strength of approximately 360 MPa [4], which is significantly lower than the average fracture strength of 2.8 GPa for monocrystalline Si [5]. This weakens the die sidewall and makes the die more susceptible to fractures initiating from the sidewall. In the lower regions of the die sidewall, the Si oxide layer separates from the sidewall leaving behind a crystalline Si surface with a peak-to-peak surface roughness in the range of 100–200 nm. In the areas where the Si oxide layer has separated from the Si die sidewall, the fracture strength of the sidewall is dependent on the material property and surface roughness of the crystalline Si, and not on the Si oxide layer [2, 3]. The mechanisms behind the Si oxide layer formation and separation during laser dicing are not well understood and have not been previously reported. 

In this work, wafer samples without and with backside Cu were diced utilizing a production nanosecond laser dicing system. The microstructure and phases formed at the die sidewall were analysed and compared in detail by energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) and nanobeam diffraction (NBD) in a transmission electron microscope (TEM). A literature survey was undertaken to further understand the microstructural differences and phase changes. Finally, a mechanism model for the Si oxide formation and separation is proposed. Understanding of these mechanisms will help to improve the quality and reliability of laser dicing of Si wafers with metal backside in the future. 

## **2  Experimental** 

Ultrathin Si wafers with frontside Cu, frontside AlCu, backside contact metallization, and backside Cu were prepared. Si trench MOSFET wafers with (001) crystal orientation were used. The mechanical and chemical thinnings of the Si wafers were carried out by temporarily mounting the wafers on rigid glass carriers using an adhesive. The cross-sectional structure of the Si wafer is illustrated in Fig. 1. The wafer frontside metallization stack consists of electroplated Cu on a sputtered AlCu (0.5 wt% Cu) layer. The laser dicing street is free from any frontside metallization. A thin film stack of Ti/NiV/Ag was sputtered on the Si wafer backside as contact metallization. A layer of backside Cu was then electroplated on the contact layer. Two types of wafers were fabricated. The first wafer type does not have any backside Cu (reference wafer), while the second type has backside Cu. The wafers were demounted from the glass carriers and simultaneously mounted on a UV tape for laser dicing. Prior to laser dicing, a layer of water-soluble polyvinyl alcohol was spin coated on the wafer frontside to trap laser dicing debris particles. The coating was washed away by pressurized water jet after laser dicing. The wafer dicing was carried out using a production laser dicing system with a 35 ns pulse duration and 355 nm (UV) wavelength under normal air atmosphere. The production settings used were optimized for manufacturing throughput and kerf quality: 10 kHz pulse repetition rate, 0.4 mJ pulse energy, 10 µm × 500 µm elliptical beam spot size, 0 mm beam defocus, and 96% pulse overlap. For nanosecond laser, high ablation rate develops in Si due to thermal vaporization at peak fluences > 3 J/ cm<sup>2</sup> [6, 7 ]. The peak fluence of the laser used for dicing the wafer samples is 6.4 J/cm<sup>2</sup> , which is approximately a factor of twice the threshold for thermal vaporization. This gives a good balance between high ablation rate and ablation quality in terms of ablation morphology such as kerf roughness, chipping severity, debris size and distribution, and depth of heat-affected zone (HAZ) [8]. Silicon oxidation at high O2 pressures (20–760 Torr) has been extensively studied from 800 to 1300 °C [20]. The slow formation of a  SiO2 layer growing according to a parabolic or logarithmic law has been observed. The studies carried out by Gulbransen et al. Gelain et al. [20] and Lander et al. [23] observed an abrupt transition between a condition of passive oxidation in which a layer of  SiO2 is formed at high  O2 pressures and low temperatures, and an active oxidation condition in which volatile SiO is formed at low  O2 pressures and high temperatures. Smith et al. [24] extended the studies of Gelain et al. [20] and Lander et al. [23] (covering a temperature range of 600–1200 °C, and a  O2 pressure range of  10<sup>–9</sup> –10<sup>–2</sup> Torr) and found the critical  O2 pressures and substrate temperatures necessary for active oxidation to be independent of Si crystal orientation and doping type. High SiO gas pressures develop at the  SiO2/Si interface at low  O2 pressures, with the  SiO2 layer being consumed by reaction with Si. When _p_ SiO _> p_ O2 , where _p_ SiO is the rapid reaction occurs with the SiO gas diffusing through pressure at the  SiO2/Si interface and _p_ O2 is the  O2 pressure, fissures or holes in the  SiO2 layer causing the separation of that layer from the crystalline Si [25]. The basic mechanisms responsible for the decomposition of a  SiO2 layer on crystalline Si have been addressed by Tromp et al. [26] and Liehr et al. [27]. In contrast to the lateral uniformity of the Si oxidation process, the  SiO2 decomposition process has been found to be highly nonuniform. As can be seen in Figs. 5b and 8c, microvoids were formed at the  SiO2/ Si interface, exposing regions of atomically clean crystalline Si, while the surrounding  SiO2 retained its initial 

thickness. It has been suggested that defects at the  SiO2/ Si interface, such as micropores [26], metal impurities [27] and Si hydroxyl groups [28], can catalyze the  SiO2 decomposition reaction and serve as nucleation points for microvoids. Self-diffusion of Si across the clean Si surface inside the microvoids supplies Si for reaction with  SiO2 at the periphery of the microvoids, where volatile SiO can be formed and causing the microvoids to increase in size [26–28]. The nonuniform microvoid nucleation, depletion of Si in the inner area of the microvoids, and accumulation of Si at the periphery of the microvoids could be responsible for the observed roughening of the  SiO2/Si interface. Figure 5b indicates that the microvoids grow laterally as the reaction proceeds, then coalesce to cause the separation of the  SiO2 layer from the crystalline Si surface. The microvoid diameter growth rate is rate limited by the peripheral reaction and is not dependent on the nature of the nucleating defect [27].

A critical phenomenon that effects the die sidewall strength is the formation and separation of a  SiO2 layer at the sidewall.
